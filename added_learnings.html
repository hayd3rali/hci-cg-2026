Lecture 1

HCI Fundamentals

Human–Computer Interaction (HCI) integrates principles from Cognitive Psychology, Visual Design, and Computer Science to analyze the relationship between users and digital systems.

Behavioral Patterns and Recall

  • Task execution is often driven by early conditioning or mental models formed during childhood (for example, retrieving visual archetypes like a cup).
  • While specific routines and habits are mutable, the core characteristics of human nature tend to persist.
  • Cognitive processing archives data within:
    • Working Memory (STM)
    • Permanent Storage (LTM)
    • Sensory Registers (Perceptual Storage)

System Interfaces and Theoretical Models

  • Types of interaction paradigms: Graphical (GUI), Command Line (CLI), Voice Control, and Motion/Gesture sensing
  • Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) mimic human visual perception by identifying features like surface texture, geometric shapes, and color spectrums.
  • Fitts’s Law helped drive the creation of the computer mouse; it calculates the time needed to acquire a target based on its width and distance.
  • Chunking facilitates memory retention by grouping extensive data strings into digestible segments (for instance, formatting 1234567890 as 123-456-7890).

UI vs. UX Design

UI (User Interface) is dedicated to the visual styling, aesthetics, and presentation of a product.

UX (User Experience) encompasses the holistic journey, focusing on how the product feels, its efficiency, and the user's overall satisfaction.

Lecture 2

The Cognitive Processing Chain

  1. Sensory Input
  2. Focused Attention
  3. Perception & Interpretation
  4. Knowledge Acquisition (Learning)
  5. Retention (Memory)
  6. Information Loss (Forgetting)
  7. Cognitive Processing (Thinking)
  8. Solution Finding
  9. Logical Reasoning
  10. Action Selection (Decision Making)

Case Study: The Automatic Syringe

The Automatic Syringe incident highlights how minor choices in interface layout can yield critical outcomes. Specifically, the implementation of numeric keypads resulted in severe medical errors caused by erroneous keystrokes.

Since initial interface designs are rarely flawless, an iterative approach is vital. Preliminary prototypes should be disposable and flexible; employing rapid prototyping methods like paper sketching and low-fidelity mock-ups allows designers to validate concepts swiftly prior to engineering.

Lecture 3

The Gulfs of Interaction

The Gulf of Execution is the disconnect between a user's intent and the capabilities provided by the system. Conversely, the Gulf of Evaluation is the difficulty a user faces in interpreting the system's state or feedback.
